Section 01

Financial Review Card Redesign

A complete rethink of how Advizmo communicates recurring financial changes to users. This component sits at the intersection of six critical financial calculations and must convey urgency without alarm, detail without clutter, and trust without complexity.

Why This Component Matters

The recurring review card is the single entry point for users to understand changes to their financial baseline. It directly affects:

Impacted Calculations

Safe-to-invest amountDepends on expenses
Cash flow forecastDepends on income/expenses
Bills coverage ratioDepends on bills total
Emergency reserve targetDepends on monthly burn
Investment recommendationsDepends on surplus
Tax optimizationDepends on deductions
Current Problems
  • Feels like a notification banner - no financial weight or seriousness
  • Only surfaces bills, ignoring income changes entirely
  • No hierarchy - a $1 Spotify increase gets the same treatment as a $44 electricity jump
  • Does not connect changes to their downstream impact on financial health
  • Users skip it because it feels like noise, not signal
Design Goals
  • 2-second understanding: users grasp the magnitude instantly
  • Trust through transparency: show what changed, why it matters
  • Premium fintech feel: not a bill reminder, a financial intelligence tool
  • Progressive disclosure: simple at first glance, detailed on demand
  • Income-aware: changes to income are just as important as expense changes

Concept 01

Financial Review Preview

Show the 2-3 most important changes directly in the card. Users see the magnitude before deciding whether to dig deeper. Leads with the biggest financial impact.

Preview-first

Philosophy

"Show, don't tell. Surface the 3 changes that matter most."

This concept assumes users want to see the numbers immediately without tapping into a detail screen. The card acts as a financial snapshot - a quick glance that tells you whether you need to take action.

The hierarchy is driven by dollar magnitude: the largest increase gets top billing. New items get a distinct visual treatment to differentiate from increases.

The financial impact section connects individual changes to the bigger picture - showing both the monthly delta and the before/after totals.

Pros and Cons
Instant comprehension Financial weight Shows impact Limited to 3 items Needs truncation logic
Best For

Users with 2-5 changes per month. Works well when changes are infrequent and users care about magnitude.

Concept 02

Financial Summary

Summarize, don't list. Lead with a 2x2 grid that gives the full financial picture at a glance. The review is a destination, not the card itself.

Summary-first

Philosophy

"Give the full picture in 4 numbers. The details live one tap away."

This concept takes a dashboard approach. Instead of listing individual changes, it summarizes the financial state into four key numbers: income, expenses, changes, and net. This is the approach most aligned with how financial advisors present information.

The card communicates financial health, not just change events. Users see whether their overall position is improving or deteriorating before diving into specifics.

The change summary line provides a quick count and the total impact, acting as a bridge between the high-level view and the detailed review screen.

Pros and Cons
Complete picture Income + expenses Scales well Less immediate detail Extra tap for specifics
Best For

Power users who want a financial dashboard. Works well for users with both income and expense changes, or who review finances weekly.

Concept 03

Expandable Review

Collapsed by default - just the headline numbers. Expanded on tap - full detail list. Progressive disclosure keeps the interface clean while making details accessible.

Progressive disclosure

Philosophy

"Compact when you don't need it. Detailed when you do."

This concept respects the user's attention by defaulting to a minimal state. The collapsed view shows just the change count and total impact - enough to gauge urgency. Tapping expands to the full list.

This is the most iOS-native approach. Apple's own apps use expandable sections extensively (Mail, Messages, Settings). It feels familiar and does not demand attention.

The trade-off is discoverability: some users may never tap to expand. The collapsed state must be compelling enough to signal that details are worth exploring.

Pros and Cons
iOS native pattern Respects attention Scales to many items Discovery risk Extra tap needed
Best For

Users who check the app daily. Works well when changes are frequent and the user wants control over information density.

Concept 04

Priority Review

Only show items that materially affect financial recommendations. A $44 electricity increase is prioritized over a $1 Spotify bump. Hierarchy signals what matters.

Impact-ranked

Philosophy

"Not all changes are equal. Show the ones that move the needle."

This concept introduces financial hierarchy into the card. The primary item gets a large, prominent treatment with full context (previous amount, new amount, percentage change). Secondary items are compact.

The design philosophy is borrowed from news editorial layout: the lead story gets a headline treatment, secondary stories get brief mentions. This helps users quickly identify what deserves their attention.

The "1 more change" expandable link keeps the card compact while signaling that more details exist. This is a middle ground between Concept 1 (all visible) and Concept 3 (all hidden).

Pros and Cons
Clear hierarchy Signals importance Editorial feel Algorithm needed Subjective ranking
Best For

Users who want guidance on what matters. Works well when the app can algorithmically rank changes by financial impact.

Concept 05

Financial Timeline

Challenge the card assumption entirely. Show a chronological view of the month's financial activity - income received, bills paid, new charges. Users see money flowing through their life.

Timeline-first

Philosophy

"Money is a story. Show the timeline, not just the balance sheet."

This concept challenges the assumption that a "card" is the right container. Instead, it presents a chronological narrative of financial activity. Each event is a moment in time - when money came in, when it went out.

The timeline approach is particularly powerful for variable income users (freelancers, rental income) because it shows the timing of cash flows, not just totals.

This is the most emotionally resonant concept. Seeing "Salary received +$6,500" next to "Rent paid -$1,800" creates a narrative of financial life that numbers alone can not convey.

Pros and Cons
Narrative flow Shows timing Emotionally rich Breaks card pattern Needs date data Scrolling required
Best For

Users with variable income/expenses. Works well as a dedicated "Activity" tab rather than a card on the dashboard.

Section 08

Recommendation

Based on the comparison analysis, one concept emerges as the strongest primary choice - with a hybrid approach that borrows the best elements from others.

Primary Recommendation

Concept 2: Financial Summary

The Financial Summary concept scores highest across all six dimensions. It provides the most complete financial picture in a single glance, scales to any number of changes, treats income as a first-class citizen, and uses a grid layout that feels native to iOS (similar to Apple Weather and Apple Health).

The 2x2 grid is the key innovation. It transforms the component from a "notification about changes" into a "financial health snapshot." Users don't just see that things changed - they see whether their overall position is improving or deteriorating.

When to Use Each Concept

Concept 1: Review Preview

Best for: Quick mobile notifications

Use when pushing a notification that needs to convey urgency. The compact format works well in push notifications and lock screen previews.

Concept 2: Financial Summary

Best for: Dashboard home screen

Use as the primary card on the home dashboard. It gives users a reason to open the app - to see their full financial picture at a glance.

Concept 3: Expandable Review

Best for: Notification center feed

Use in notification center or activity feeds where multiple review events may stack. The collapsed state keeps feeds tidy.

Concept 4: Priority Review

Best for: Large change sets (10+)

Use when the algorithm detects high-impact changes that need immediate attention. The editorial hierarchy guides focus.

Concept 5: Financial Timeline

Best for: Dedicated Activity tab

Use as a full-screen view in the Activity tab, not as a card. The narrative format works best with vertical scrolling space.

Hybrid Approach

Best for: Complete experience

Concept 2 on home + Concept 3 in notifications + Concept 5 in Activity tab. Each serves a different user intent.

Hybrid Architecture

Recommended Implementation

Home DashboardConcept 2 - Financial Summary
Push NotificationsConcept 1 - Review Preview (compact)
Notification CenterConcept 3 - Expandable Review
Activity TabConcept 5 - Financial Timeline

Section 10

UX Writing Guide

The language of financial components must be calm, intelligent, and trustworthy. Not robotic. Not marketing. Not technical. Here's how to write for this component.

Before and After

Recurring bills->Monthly Money Review
Subscriptions changed->4 changes this month
Review bills->Review all changes
Stay on top of spending->Keep your cash flow accurate
Bill alert->Monthly review
New charge detected->New recurring expense

Component Labels

Total bills->Total expenses
Monthly cost->/month
View details->Review details
Dismiss->Maybe later
Your balance->Net cash flow
Transactions->Activity

Impact Descriptions

Your bill went up->Increased from $98 to $142
Price increase->+$44/mo
New subscription added->New $49.99/mo expense
You're spending more->Monthly impact: +$97.49
Budget alert->Your expenses went from $2,307 to $2,405
Action required->Review all changes

Writing Principles

Calm, intelligent, trustworthy

Write as if a trusted financial advisor is speaking. Not a robot. Not a salesperson. Someone who respects the user's intelligence.

Lead with what changed

"Electricity increased from $98 to $142" not "Your electricity bill has been updated." Numbers first, context second.

Show numbers, not just descriptions

"+$97.49" is more useful than "a significant increase." Specificity builds trust.

Never alarm, always inform

"Monthly impact: +$97.49" not "Warning: your costs are spiraling." Financial decisions require calm, not panic.

Tone Spectrum

Wrong Tone (Avoid)

Alarmist"URGENT: Your bills increased!"
Robotic"Bill modification detected"
Marketing"Stay on top of your spending!"
Technical"Recurring payment variance noted"
