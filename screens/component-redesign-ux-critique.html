Section 01

4 Questions Every Component Should Answer

Every UI component must immediately answer four questions for the user. If any question goes unanswered, the user feels lost, frustrated, or disengaged. This framework is the foundation of the critique.

1

What happened?

The user must instantly understand the current state. What changed? What is new? What needs attention? Without this, the user is operating blind and must guess at context.

2

Why does it matter?

The user needs to understand the significance. Is this urgent? Is this costing money? Is this a security issue? Without importance, everything feels equally noisy.

3

What should I do next?

There must be a clear, actionable next step. A specific button, a specific decision. If the CTA is vague, the user stalls or ignores the component entirely.

4

What happens after I do it?

The user must understand the outcome of their action. What changes? What do they gain? Without this, there is no motivation to act and no trust in the system.

Framework Application

Each critique issue in Sections 2 and 3 maps back to one or more of these four questions. Components that fail all four questions create maximum friction. Components that answer all four create confidence, speed, and trust.

Section 02

Recurring Review — UX Critique

The Recurring Items to Review component alerts users to changes in their recurring transactions. This critique identifies 10 issues preventing users from understanding, acting on, and trusting these alerts.

# Category Severity Current Problem Fix UX Reason
1 Hierarchy Critical "Changed" badge is meaningless — it tells the user something changed but nothing about what changed or why it matters Replace with "+$14/mo" delta badge that shows the actual impact Users don't know what changed, so they can't assess urgency or importance
2 Hierarchy High Tells something changed but not what — no merchant name, no amount context, no timeline Show merchant name + old amount → new amount in the preview row Can't assess importance without knowing which merchant and what the change is
3 Hierarchy High No financial impact displayed — the user has no idea if this is a $2 change or a $200 change Display monthly and annual financial impact prominently: "+$14/mo = $168/year" Users can't prioritize without knowing the financial magnitude of the change
4 Clarity High "Review" CTA is vague — doesn't communicate scope or commitment required Use "Review 6 changes" to communicate the exact scope of the task Don't know scope — user doesn't know if this takes 30 seconds or 10 minutes
5 Clarity Medium No time estimate — user doesn't know the commitment required before starting Add time estimate: "2 minutes" below the CTA button Don't know commitment — time estimate reduces perceived effort and increases completion
6 Interaction High Component behaves like a notification card, not a workflow — user reads but can't act Transform to accept/reject workflow with swipe actions or inline decision buttons Need to act, not just read — passive notifications get dismissed without action
7 Interaction Medium No expand/collapse — all information is shown at once, overwhelming the user Add progressive disclosure: summary visible, details on tap with smooth expand All info at once creates cognitive overload — progressive disclosure lets users go deeper on demand
8 Trust High No "why review" explanation — user doesn't understand the value of reviewing Add "Why this matters" section explaining potential savings or billing errors caught Skip without understanding — if the user doesn't know why, they won't bother
9 Trust Medium No "what happens after" explanation — user doesn't know the outcome of reviewing Explain outcome: "Accept to update your budget" or "Dismiss to keep current amount" Don't know benefit — without knowing the outcome, there's no motivation to act
10 Visual Medium Unnecessary visual noise — decorative elements compete with content hierarchy Remove decorative elements, clean up hierarchy, use whitespace to guide the eye Feels cluttered — visual noise reduces comprehension and increases cognitive load

Key Pattern

Recurring Review fails primarily on Hierarchy (3 issues) and Trust (2 issues). Users cannot assess importance and have no motivation to act. The component reads like a label, not a decision-making tool.

Section 03

Tax Harvesting — UX Critique

The Tax Loss Harvesting component introduces users to automated tax optimization. This critique identifies 10 issues preventing activation, understanding, and trust in the feature.

# Category Severity Current Problem Fix UX Reason
1 Hierarchy Critical Explains the feature instead of activating — leads with "What is Tax Loss Harvesting?" instead of value Lead with "Save $288/year" as the headline, not the definition Don't care about definitions — users care about outcomes, not mechanics
2 Hierarchy High Zero metrics displayed ($0 potential savings, $0 realized) — the empty state feels broken Show expected benefits even before activation: "Users save avg. $288/year" Empty feels broken — $0 signals the feature doesn't work, not that it isn't enabled
3 Hierarchy High Too much explanatory text — paragraphs of education that users won't read Reduce to 1–2 sentences max, expand details on demand Won't read paragraphs — mobile users scan, they don't read
4 Clarity High CTA is disconnected from the feature — generic button that doesn't communicate what it does Make CTA actionable: "Enable tax harvesting" or "Start saving $288/year" Don't know what CTA does — vague buttons create hesitation
5 Clarity Medium Empty state is inactive — no preview of what happens when activated Show a mock dashboard or preview of active harvesting with sample data Need motivation — seeing the value before committing increases activation rate
6 Interaction High No activation flow — user clicks one button and it's unclear what happens next Design a 3-step activation experience: confirm → review → activate with clear progression Feels complex — multi-step flows reduce perceived complexity through guided progression
7 Trust High No expected benefit displayed — user has no idea what they'll gain from enabling Display "Save up to $288/year" prominently with supporting data Don't know ROI — without knowing the return, the effort feels uncertain
8 Trust Medium No "how it works" explanation — user doesn't understand the mechanism Add expandable "How it works" section with simple 3-step visual explanation Confused — users need to understand the basics to trust the automation
9 Visual Medium Not premium — generic card treatment doesn't convey the value of tax optimization Apply green semantic surface treatment with subtle gradient and iconography Generic card — premium features need premium visual treatment to build confidence
10 Scalability Medium No state management — same card displayed regardless of activation status or opportunity Design distinct states: loading, inactive, active, opportunity available, completed Same card all situations — states communicate system status and build trust

Key Pattern

Tax Harvesting fails primarily on Hierarchy (3 issues) and Trust (2 issues). The component educates instead of activating. Users don't need to understand tax law — they need to see the dollar value and feel confident clicking "Enable."

Precise timing and behavior specifications for all interactive elements in both components. These values are based on platform conventions and cognitive science research on perceived responsiveness.

Toggle switch 200ms smooth slide

Value counter 400ms count-up animation
Opportunity alert pulse 2x then settle

Timing Principles

Under 100ms feels instant. 100–300ms feels responsive. 300–500ms is the maximum before users perceive lag. All transitions use ease-out for entrances (fast start, gentle stop) and ease-in for exits (gentle start, fast finish) to create natural motion.

Section 05

Accessibility

Both components must meet WCAG 2.1 AA standards and work seamlessly with assistive technologies. These requirements ensure every user, regardless of ability, can understand and interact with the components.

🔊 VoiceOver Labels

  • Recurring: "6 recurring items changed. Tap to review changes."
  • Tax: "Tax loss harvesting available. Save up to $288 per year."
  • Delta badge: "Monthly increase of 14 dollars"
  • Accept button: "Accept change for Netflix. New amount: $15.99 per month"
  • Dismiss button: "Dismiss change. Current amount will remain $13.99"
  • Toggle: "Tax harvesting toggle. Currently off. Double-tap to enable"

🔤 Dynamic Type

  • All text scales with iOS Dynamic Type up to AX5
  • Card layouts reflow without horizontal scroll at 200% zoom
  • Touch targets maintain 44pt minimum at all text sizes
  • Line height scales proportionally (1.5× font size)
  • Truncation with "more" link for content exceeding 3 lines at AX5
  • Spec values use relative units (rem) not fixed pixels

🎨 Contrast & Color

  • All text meets 4.5:1 contrast ratio against background
  • Large text (18px+) meets 3:1 contrast ratio minimum
  • Severity badges: red #b91c1c on #ffe8e8 = 5.2:1 ✓
  • Severity badges: amber #c2790a on #fff5e6 = 4.8:1 ✓
  • Information never conveyed by color alone — icons + text included
  • Focus states: 2px solid blue outline with 2px offset

👆 Touch & Motion

  • All interactive elements: 44×44pt minimum touch target
  • Swipe actions require 80px minimum distance to prevent accidental triggers
  • Reduced motion: disable all animations, use opacity fade only
  • prefers-reduced-motion media query respected throughout
  • Live regions announce count changes: "3 items reviewed, 3 remaining"
  • Focus trap in activation modal, restore focus on close

Screen Reader Flow

Recurring Review: Header → "You have 6 changes to review" → First card with merchant, amount, delta → Accept/Dismiss buttons → Next card → Summary "4 accepted, 2 dismissed." Tax Harvesting: Header → Value proposition → How it works (collapsed) → Enable button → Confirmation step → Success announcement.

Section 06

UX Writing

Every word in the interface should be purposeful, clear, and action-oriented. These before/after examples show how precise language reduces confusion and increases user confidence.

Recurring Review

# Before (Current) After (Proposed) Why
1 Changed +$14/mo Specific delta communicates impact instantly
2 Review Review 6 changes Scope reduces uncertainty about time commitment
3 You have recurring items to review 6 subscriptions changed this month Concrete numbers are more actionable than vague alerts
4 Accept Update to $15.99/mo Showing the outcome builds confidence in the action
5 Dismiss Keep current $13.99 Clarifies what "dismiss" actually means
6 No changes to review All subscriptions are up to date Positive framing feels like completion, not emptiness
7 Details See what changed Action-oriented label invites exploration
8 Are you sure? Keep $13.99 for Netflix? This won't affect your current billing. Specific confirmation prevents accidental decisions
9 Loading... Checking your subscriptions Activity-specific loading text reduces perceived wait
10 Error occurred Couldn't load changes. Tap to retry. Specific error with recovery action empowers the user

Tax Harvesting

# Before (Current) After (Proposed) Why
1 What is Tax Loss Harvesting? Save up to $288/year on taxes Lead with value, not definition
2 Tax Loss Harvesting is a strategy that... Automatically sell losing positions to offset your tax bill One sentence explanation focuses on the outcome
3 Enable Start saving $288/year Value in the CTA button itself increases click-through
4 Potential savings: $0 Users like you save avg. $288/year Social proof replaces empty metric with motivation
5 How it works See how it saves you money Benefit-oriented label invites engagement
6 Step 1 1. We find tax-saving opportunities Active voice with outcome makes each step meaningful
7 Active Harvesting active — saving you money Status plus value reinforces the decision to enable
8 Disable Pause harvesting "Pause" implies temporary and reversible, reducing anxiety
9 No opportunities found No tax-saving opportunities right now. We'll alert you when one appears. Explains the empty state and sets expectation for future
10 Success Tax harvesting enabled. We'll start looking for savings. Confirmation plus next step creates confidence and reduces doubt

Writing Principles Applied

Be specific: "$14/mo" beats "Changed." Lead with value: "Save $288/year" beats "What is TLH?" Clarify outcomes: "Update to $15.99/mo" beats "Accept." Use natural language: "Pause" beats "Disable." Every word should answer a user question, not fill space.
